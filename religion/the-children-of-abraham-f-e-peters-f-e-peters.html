In this pioneering book, F. E. Peters, a scholar without peer in the comparative study of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, traces the shared origins, development, and features of these religions. Direct, accessible, and nuanced, The Children of Abraham chronicles the development of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam from the sixth-century B.C., when the Jews returned to Palestine from exile in Babylonia, to the Middle Ages, when each of the three faiths approached its present form. The book shows what members of these faith groups, whom Muslims refer to as "People of the Book," have in common, including, most notably, that each worships a God whom its adherents believe intervenes in history on behalf of His people. First published in 1982, and thoroughly revised in 2004, Children of Abraham is an essential book for understanding what binds these religions together. Complete with a new preface by the author, this Princeton Classics edition presents this landmark study to a new generation of readers.
